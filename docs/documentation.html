Getting started

Freally Capture is in development. As of the 1.0.0 stable, you can build scenes on the GPU compositor, mix audio across up to 6 tracks (including per-application audio), record multi-track to disk, go live (single-target and simultaneous multistream over RTMP/RTMPS/SRT/WHIP) with Studio Mode, a replay buffer, vertical/multi-canvas, nested scenes, and a no-key chat overlay — extend it all with the remote-control API, browser docks, Lua scripting, and a plugin SDK — and keep it current with a signed self-hosted auto-updater. The preview is a native GPU surface ("OBS feel") on Windows, macOS, and Linux. The interface speaks 18 languages, is fully keyboard-operable and screen-reader-audible, sets itself up with a first-run wizard, and can be themed light, dark, or custom. The first CAP-M must-have batch (0.97.0) adds scene-editing depth — multi-step undo/redo, an OBS scene-collection importer, a missing-file doctor, a multi-select with align/distribute and custom guides, copy-paste transforms, a keying workbench — and monitoring — a multiview and fullscreen projectors for any scene/source/program/preview on any display, plus a one-key still grab. The second batch (0.98.0) adds broadcast safety & reliability: a go-live pre-flight checklist, always-on safety alarms, a source health dashboard, mid-session encoder failover, crash-safe recording with a next-launch repair, a quit guard with an ordered shutdown, a panic button, recording filename templates, and a redacted diagnostics bundle — see Broadcast safety. The third and final batch (0.99.0) closes the must-have set with sources, devices & calibration: built-in test signals (SMPTE bars, calibration grid, motion sweep, lineup tone, A/V flash+beep), a guided A/V sync calibration workbench, timer & clock sources, text bound to a watched file (.txt/CSV/JSON), a searchable hotkey map with conflict detection, mixer pan/solo/mono, per-device deinterlacing, and cross-platform camera controls with per-device profiles — see Sources & devices. The automation + capture & device-depth release (0.200.0) makes the studio drive itself and finishes the glass-to-canvas path: an automation rules engine, macros with variables & sequences, MIDI and OSC control surfaces, hotkey chords & layers, a LAN touch panel and a tally-light service, PTZ camera control, and a show rundown — plus a low-latency passthrough monitor, pixel-perfect scaling, punch-in zoom & follow pan, auto black-bar crop, window↔app-audio auto-linking, and HDR→SDR tone-mapping, alongside a scene backdrop and true reverse media playback. Every automation surface routes through one fixed, off-by-default command allowlist, so none can name a file or reach the internet. The new-sources & overlays release (0.300.0) puts ten new things on the canvas: an instant-replay playback source (roll the armed buffer into the program at 100/50/25 % — retimed, never interpolated), a truly gapless media playlist with per-item trims and cue points, a layered title & scoreboard designer with live control, an audio visualizer (FFT bars, oscilloscope, stereo VU) tapped post-fader, a system-stats overlay of real measured numbers, a LiveSplit-compatible speedrun split timer, a keyboard/mouse/gamepad input overlay, cursor highlight & click effects, an SRT/RTMP LAN ingest listener with a QR-coded URL, and Freally Link — instance-to-instance program sharing over your own network, gated by a pairing key, with no ffmpeg on either end. Both LAN sources are off until added, bind only the local machine, and never touch the internet; the input overlay and cursor effects read input only while enabled and never log a keystroke. The compositor & FX depth release (0.310.0 → 0.400.0) adds 3D corner-pin, downstream keyer layers, freeze-frame, source cloning, an expanded blur family, a move (morph) transition, per-scene-pair transition rules, a WGSL shader studio, a bezier mask editor, and track-matte stingers with an optional program-audio duck. The audio production depth release (0.500.0) turns the mixer into a broadcast console: physical output routing and aux/cue mixes, a ducking matrix, a gain-sharing auto-mixer, EBU R128 loudness normalization, a parametric EQ over a live spectrum, a de-esser and rumble guard, a soundboard, audio-only (podcast) recording, per-guest mix-minus with voice-chain presets, and CLAP + VST3 plugin discovery — every audio filter now a live visual instrument with in/out/gain-reduction meters, all owned classic DSP, off by default. The recording & replay depth release (0.600.0) builds out what happens to the file: per-source ISO recording (clean per-source and per-guest files, timecode-aligned to the program), a replay & clip trimmer with a stream-copy-vs-re-encode badge and a one-click 9:16 reframe, alpha-channel recording to the owned lossless format with ProRes 4444 / QTRLE master export, event-driven split on scene change / marker / rundown step, typed auto-markers from studio events, a closed sandboxed post-record pipeline (verify → remux → normalize → rename → move/copy → reveal → notify scripts), a recording integrity verifier, and owned SMPTE LTC timecode generate/read with a burn-in overlay. See Teleprompter.

Status: stable. 1.0.0 completes the 100-feature roadmap — capture, the GPU compositor + filters, the audio mixer, multi-track recording, the native GPU preview, single-target + multistream streaming with Studio Mode, the extensibility surface (remote API, docks, scripting, plugins), signed auto-update, all 26 must-haves (0.97.0–0.99.0), the depth phases (automation, sources & overlays, compositor FX, audio production, recording & replay, streaming reliability, remote guests, studio management, the teleprompter rework), and the launch polish (the Starting Soon countdown, background music, the social bar, the featured chat banner). The virtual camera, a true browser source, and game-capture GPU hooking follow as their own milestones — download builds from the Releases page.

Windows / macOS / Linux

  1. Download your platform's installer from the Releases page (Windows .msi/.exe, macOS .dmg, Linux AppImage/.deb/.rpm/Flatpak) and install it. The Windows build is a GUI app with no console window.
  2. Launch Freally Capture. It opens to a clean studio — a program preview, and Scenes, Sources, Mixer, and Controls docks. No account, no login.
  3. On macOS, grant Screen Recording (and Camera/Microphone if you'll use them) in System Settings → Privacy & Security, then relaunch. On Linux (Wayland), capture goes through the desktop portal — you'll pick the source to share.
  4. Add your first source (see "Build a scene") and you're ready to record or go live.

Build a scene

A scene is a stack of sources composed on the GPU. Sources can be shared across scenes.

  1. In the Sources rail, click Add Source and pick a type: Display, Window, Game, Webcam / Capture Card, Browser, Media, Image, Text, Color, or Audio.
  2. Add a Display Capture — the program preview fills with your screen.
  3. Add a Webcam source, then drag it on the canvas to a corner and resize it with the handles.
  4. Add an Image or Browser overlay on top. Reorder sources in the rail to set their z-order (top of the list = front).
  5. Create more scenes in the Scenes rail (e.g. "Starting Soon", "Gameplay", "BRB") and switch between them.
Tip: A source added to one scene can be reused in others — change its properties once and it updates everywhere it appears.
Real-time preview: on Windows, the program preview is painted straight from the GPU compositor ("OBS feel") — drag, scale, and rotate feel instant, with the selection box and handles drawn right into the GPU frame. macOS and Linux use the same on-canvas editing over the standard preview while the native path lands there next.

Filters & transitions

Stack per-source filters on the GPU, and choose how scenes cut to each other.

Add a chroma key

  1. Right-click a source (e.g. a green-screen webcam or overlay) and choose Filters.
  2. Add Chroma Key. Adjust key color, similarity, smoothness, and spill — the keyed color vanishes live on the canvas.
  3. Add more filters as needed (Color Correction, LUT, Blur, Mask, Sharpen). Drag to reorder the chain.

Set a transition

  1. In Controls, open the transition picker and choose Cut, Fade, Slide, Swipe, Stinger, or Luma Wipe, and set a duration.
  2. For a Stinger, pick the media and the transition point; for a Luma Wipe, pick the gradient and softness.
  3. Switch scenes to see the transition play. In Studio Mode, the transition runs when you commit Preview → Program.

Compositor & visual FX

The compositor depth from 0.310.0 and 0.400.0 — 3D tilt, downstream keyers, freeze and clone, an expanded blur family, motion transitions with rules, a shader studio, bezier masks, and track-matte stingers.

3D tilt (corner-pin) & perspective

  1. Open Edit Transform on an item and set Tilt X, Tilt Y, and a Perspective strength to pin the source in 3D. An untilted transform renders pixel-identically — the projective matrix kicks in only when a tilt is applied.

Downstream keyer (DSK) layers

  1. Open Tools → Downstream Keyers and add any source as a persistent overlay composited on the program output, above every scene and surviving scene cuts — a station logo, a LIVE badge, a lower-third.
  2. Reorder layers and set opacity, position/size, and on/off per layer. They render into preview, recording, stream, and multiview alike.

Freeze-frame & source cloning

  1. Right-click a source, open Filters, and add Freeze — it holds the last frame across program, preview, recording, and stream together; toggle it off to unfreeze.
  2. Use the Clone button (⧉) on a source row to place one feed in multiple scenes or spots, each clone carrying its own transform and filter stack.

Blur family

  1. In a source's Filters, add one of the GPU blurs beside the Gaussian: directional (motion streak along an angle), radial (spin about a center), zoom (dolly-zoom streak), or pixelate (mosaic).

Move (morph) transition & transition rules

  1. Choose the Move transition: items present in both the outgoing and incoming scene animate from their old position/scale/rotation/crop to the new one instead of cutting, while items in only one scene fade in or out (eased with a smootherstep).
  2. Open Tools → Transition Rules to give a specific scene A→B pair its own transition kind and duration instead of the default. For per-item entrances, set a Reveal fade-in under Edit Transform so an item ramps in when it becomes visible.

WGSL shader studio

  1. Add a user WGSL effect as a filter and edit it live; sliders are auto-generated from // @param annotations, and a built-in gallery ships grayscale, invert, scanlines, and vignette.
Tip: Shaders are compiled and validated at runtime — an invalid shader is simply ignored and the source renders unfiltered, never a crash.

Bezier mask

  1. Add the freehand mask filter and draw a closed path — drag the handles, double-click to add a point, right-click to remove — with preset shapes, per-edge feather, and invert.
  2. Export any path as a grayscale luma-wipe pattern to reuse the shape as a reveal transition.

Track-matte stinger transitions

  1. Configure a stinger to carry its transparency as a track matte, with fill and matte packed side-by-side or stacked, so per-pixel alpha survives codecs that drop it (H.264/HEVC). Optionally duck the program audio under the stinger's own audio while it plays.

Overlays & extra sources

Ten local canvas sources added in 0.300.0 — replay, playlists, titles, meters, HUDs, timers, input overlays, and two LAN feeds — all added from the Sources rail's + / Add Source menu.

Instant-replay playback

  1. Arm the replay buffer first (the Arm Replay control in the Controls dock), then from + / Add Source choose Instant Replay and drop it into a scene.
  2. Roll the last N seconds into the program with your replay hotkey — captured as a stream copy (no re-encode) and played back at 100%, 50%, or 25%, retimed rather than frame-interpolated. Scrub or pause while it plays.
  3. At the end the source clears itself back to transparent, so the show returns to live on its own.
Tip: Slow-motion playback is silent by design — retimed audio would smear, so it is dropped rather than faked.

Media playlist & background music

  1. Add Media Playlist (gapless), then build an ordered list with per-item in/out trims and cue points. The whole trimmed list runs through one decode, so item boundaries are frame-exact and truly gapless.
  2. Set the run mode — loop, shuffle, or hold-last — and step with the next/previous hotkeys. An audio-only playlist shows the current track's name on the canvas and plays through its own mixer strip — click that strip's Monitor button to hear it in preview.
  3. For invisible looping background music, use + / Add Source → Background Music… instead: pick one or more .mp3/.wav/.flac files (optionally shuffled). It plays with no on-canvas card and mutes a video backdrop's own sound, so only one thing is ever heard.

Title & scoreboard designer

  1. Add Title / Scoreboard to open the layered designer: stack text, image, and shape layers, with outline and drop-shadow text and animate-in/out (fade, slide, wipe).
  2. Bind fields to a watched file (a CSV cell or JSON pointer) or a studio variable, then control it live — swap a name, edit the score, or fire the animation without touching the scene.

Audio visualizer

  1. Add Audio Visualizer and pick a style — FFT spectrum bars, an oscilloscope, or stereo VU meters — then bind it to any mixer strip, track bus, or the master mix. It also visualizes a backdrop video's audio.
  2. In the source's Properties, toggle Classic colors for the classic VU/spectrum green→yellow→red bands, or leave it off for a single solid color; size, bar count, and fall rate live there too.
Tip: The visualizer is tapped post-fader, so a muted source visualizes flat — it shows exactly what you hear.

Stats HUD, split timer & input overlay

  1. Add Performance Stats (HUD) to composite the Stats dock's real measured numbers — fps, CPU, memory, GPU compose time, dropped frames, and live bitrate — with per-line toggles (GPU utilization is deliberately absent, since it isn't measured).
  2. Add Speedrun Split Timer, import a LiveSplit .lss file, and run it with global split/undo/skip/reset hotkeys against personal best, best segments, or average. The file is read only — a run is never written back.
  3. Add Input Overlay (keys/pad) for a live keyboard, mouse, and gamepad view in one of four layouts (WASD, compact keyboard, dual-stick pad, 8-way fight stick). Keyboard/mouse are Windows-first; gamepads work on all three OSes.

Social & channels bar

  1. Add Social Bar from + / Add Source: a tidy panel listing your handles — pick a platform per row (YouTube, Twitch, Kick, X, Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, Discord as brand-coloured badges, or Custom with your own label and colour) and type the handle (e.g. @mychannel). Add an optional header line ("Follow me"), reorder rows, and set the text size and colours.
  2. Every field stays editable afterwards in the source's Properties; rows with an empty handle are skipped, so you can keep a full list and fill in only what you use.

Featured chat message banner

  1. Open Tools → Featured Chat Message… (or the command palette). The picker lists the newest lines from every running chat overlay — click Feature on one to pin it to the program as a styled bottom banner that viewers of the stream and recording see.
  2. Set the banner and text colours right in the dialog (they persist); click Clear to take the banner off air. The pin itself clears on restart, by design — yesterday's shout-out never haunts today's show.

Cursor highlight & click effects

  1. Right-click a Display or Window capture and open Filters, then add Cursor effects: a Cursor halo (color and radius), color-coded left/right Click ripples, and optional Keystroke ghosting near the pointer. Changes apply live.
Note: Cursor effects are Windows-only — macOS and Linux composite the cursor OS-side. Both the input overlay and keystroke ghosting read input only while enabled and are drawn straight into the frame; nothing is ever stored or logged.

LAN sources: ingest & Freally Link

  1. Add LAN Ingest (SRT/RTMP listener) to open a listener on this machine; point a phone camera app, encoder, or second PC at the URL shown (with a QR code) and it lands in the scene with its own mixer strip. SRT can carry an optional AES passphrase; RTMP has no authentication of its own, so the form prefers SRT.
  2. For instance-to-instance sharing, on the sending machine enable the Freally Link output and set its pairing key (the output cannot be enabled without one). On the receiving machine, add Freally Link (another instance), hit Scan the LAN, and type the key — the sender's program appears as a source. No ffmpeg is needed on either end, and a receiver that can't present the key never sees a frame.
Note: Both LAN sources are off until you add them, bind only the local machine, and never touch the internet — nothing discovers you and nothing dials out.

Starting Soon countdown

A one-click pre-show holding screen — a big countdown to showtime over an image, GIF, video, gradient, or music backdrop — that flashes at zero and waits for you to cut to live.

Add the countdown

  1. In the Sources rail, open + / Add Source and choose Starting Soon…. It drops a full-canvas countdown — a caption above a big number — onto the scene.
  2. Type the caption (e.g. "Starting Soon", "Be Right Back"), then set the target with the clock picker: an hour (1–12), a minute in five-minute steps, and AM/PM — the source counts down TO that wall-clock time.
  3. At zero the countdown flashes and holds at 0:00:00 — it never auto-switches. Clear it and cut to your live scene by hand when you're ready. Every field stays editable later in the source's Properties.

Choose a backdrop

  1. Pick the fill: a two-color Gradient (the default), a Solid color, an Image (click Browse… for a file dialog), or Transparent to let a source underneath show through.
  2. Choose a GIF or video file and it plays as the scene's looping backdrop behind the numbers — stretched to fit and centered, so the whole picture is visible and never cropped.
  3. Add background music right from the dialog's Browse… (mp3/wav/flac/m4a/aac/ogg/opus) — it loops, is heard in preview, and is captured to your recording or stream. It plays with no visible card, and a video backdrop's own sound is muted so only one thing is ever heard.
Tip: Want music behind a gradient or transparent screen with no video at all? Leave the backdrop on Gradient or Transparent and add the music — a clean countdown with a soundtrack.

Audio mixer

Mix every audio source, route to up to 6 tracks, and clean up your mic — all live.

  1. Each audio source appears as a channel strip in the Mixer — name, VU meter, fader, mute, monitor, filters, and track dots (1–6).
  2. Drag a fader to set level; click mute or monitor as needed; assign the source to any subset of the 6 tracks.
  3. Open a source's filters and add Denoise (classic DSP — no ML), Noise Gate, Compressor, Limiter, EQ, or Gain.
  4. Bind a push-to-talk or push-to-mute hotkey; watch the LUFS meter for program loudness.
macOS note: macOS doesn't hand apps "what you hear" on its own. Use ScreenCaptureKit audio (macOS 13+) where available, or install a virtual device such as BlackHole and select it as your system-audio source. Freally Capture guides you and keeps recording even if a source can't open.

Record

Record your program feed multi-track with your GPU's hardware encoder, or fully lossless in the owned codec.

  1. In Settings → Encoder / Recording, pick an encoder. Freally Capture detects NVENC / Quick Sync / AMF / VAAPI / VideoToolbox and selects the best; x264 (CPU) is always available as a fallback.
  2. Choose a container — mp4 / mkv / mov / webm — or the owned freally-video lossless format (no external tool needed).
  3. Assign sources to audio tracks (up to 6) so you can separate mic, system, and guests in post. Optionally enable file splitting and record a separate-track local copy while streaming.
  4. Click Start Recording in Controls. A green REC dot shows the duration and track count. Click Stop to finalize the file.
Codec note: mp4 with H.264/AAC (and HEVC/AV1) uses patent-encumbered codecs provided by ffmpeg, which is not bundled — Freally Capture fetches it on demand and verifies it the first time you need it. The owned freally-video lossless path needs nothing fetched.

Recording & replay depth

Everything around the file: isolated per-source recordings, a clip trimmer, real transparency, event-driven splitting and markers, an after-recording task pipeline, an integrity check, and SMPTE timecode. Every capability here is off or neutral by default, and none of it adds a network call.

Per-source ISO recording

  1. Open Settings → Output and find ISO recording. Toggle Record "{name}" as its own ISO file for each source you want captured clean — each records pre-composite, at canvas size and frame rate, so every file drops onto an editing timeline aligned with the program. Because each remote guest is just a source, per-guest ISOs come for free.
  2. Leave Record with the source's filters checked to capture the processed look, or uncheck it for the raw source, and pick each lane's own ISO format and ISO video encoder. Audio is tapped post-filter but pre-fader/mute, so a mix decision never silences an ISO file.
Tip: Two ISO lanes are comfortable on a mid-range GPU; each additional lane costs another render and encode, and the panel says so up front.

Replay & clip trimmer

  1. Open a saved recording in the Trim window from the recordings list, then step the playhead with Back one frame / Forward one frame and set your points with Set in and Set out while watching the live preview.
  2. Read the export badge before you commit: a green "Exports without re-encoding" means the in-point sits on a keyframe (near-instant, lossless), while the amber badge warns it will re-encode — press Keyframe to snap the in-point for a lossless cut. Click Export clip, or use the 9:16 button to export a reframed vertical master through the vertical-canvas geometry.

Alpha-channel recording

  1. In Settings → Output, enable Record with transparency (alpha) to record the program over a transparent background to the owned .frec. The recorder gets its own transparent render, so your preview and stream keep the normal opaque program. From the recordings list, export a ProRes 4444 or QuickTime Animation (QTRLE) .mov master for your editor — MP4/MKV flatten the alpha.
Note: The .frec format version is unchanged — alpha is just a header flag, so alpha files stay playable everywhere and older files read back opaque.

Splitting & auto-markers

  1. Under the split settings for the owned .frec, choose to start a new part file on a scene switch, a marker, or a rundown step. Each part begins on the event's exact frame boundary, with a one-second minimum part length so an event storm can't produce confetti files.
  2. Turn on Drop chapter markers automatically on studio events to log scene switches, replay saves, reconnects, dropped-frame bursts, alarms, and rule actions as typed chapter markers — they ride into the mkv chapter titles and the .chapters.txt sidecar, alongside your manual marker hotkey.

Post-record pipeline & integrity verifier

  1. Build a per-profile Post-record pipeline from the closed, sandboxed action set — Verify, Remux to MP4, Normalize loudness, Rename, Move to folder / Copy to folder, Reveal in file manager, and Notify Lua scripts. It runs in the background with a live queue and stops at the first failure; there is deliberately no free-form "run a command" step.
  2. Run the recording integrity verifier (after finalize or on demand) for a plain-language OK / warning / fail report covering Container, Video continuity, Audio continuity, A/V interleave, and Duration. The owned .frec gets the deep pass; wire files get an honest ffmpeg structure and decode-error scan.

Auto-export a shareable copy on stop

  1. In Settings → Output (with the container set to the owned .frec), tick Also save a shareable MP4 next to the master. When a recording finalizes, a background export writes a double-click-playable .mp4 beside the master — you keep the lossless, crash-salvageable .frec and get a file you can hand to anyone without thinking about export.
  2. The copy appears in the post-record queue, is named collision-safe next to the master, and never touches the master itself. Off by default.

SMPTE LTC timecode

  1. Open SMPTE Timecode (LTC), enable Generate LTC onto a track and pick the mixer track and frame rate to feed external recorders and cameras; set Read LTC from an audio input to drive a burn-in timecode line on the system-stats overlay and stamp LTC chapter markers. Classic audio timecode, fully offline, round-trip accurate to ±1 frame at 24/25/30 fps.

Go live

Broadcast direct to a platform over RTMP/RTMPS — your stream goes straight from your machine, never through a server we run.

  1. Open Settings → Stream and click Add target. Pick Twitch, YouTube, Kick, Facebook, Trovo, or Custom RTMP.
  2. Paste your stream key (stored locally, masked) and set the bitrate/encoder for that service.
  3. Click Go Live in Controls. The LIVE indicator glows and the stats dock shows your bitrate, dropped frames, and stream health.
  4. If your connection blips, Freally Capture auto-reconnects. Your local recording keeps running regardless of stream state. Set a stream delay if you want one.
  5. Click End Stream to stop.

Broadcast safety

The 0.98.0 batch is about not losing the show — before, during, and after.

Before: the go-live pre-flight

Clicking Go Live first runs the checklist: stream targets keyed, a usable encoder detected, every source healthy, and enough disk for the recording — each green/red with a one-click fix — plus honest nudges for microphone/desktop-audio metering and the replay buffer (those never block a screen-only show). An optional setting holds Go Live until every blocking check is green; otherwise "Go Live anyway" is available.

During: alarms, health, and failover

  1. Safety alarms watch while output runs: silent program audio, sustained clipping, a black or frozen program picture, and a low-disk forecast ("~22 min left at the current bitrate"). They appear as a dismissible banner (and are announced to screen readers) — never a modal, never an interruption to the pipeline.
  2. The source health dashboard (palette → "Source health…") lists every source's state, fps, last-frame age, dropped frames, and restart history, with per-source Restart and Properties.
  3. If the hardware encoder faults mid-stream or mid-recording (a driver reset, GPU contention), the encoder failover hot-swaps to the other hardware family or to x264. The stream reconnects and stays up; the recording continues in a new file; a toast + a sticky stats-dock note say exactly what happened. Network faults never trigger a swap.
  4. The panic button (a bindable global hotkey) instantly cuts program — and the vertical canvas and any projectors — to a configurable privacy slate, stops every capture, and hard-mutes all audio (the microphone stops being captured at all) while the stream and recording stay connected. Restoring is a deliberate two-step confirm. Configure the slate colour/image beside the hotkey in Settings → Hotkeys.

After: crash-safe recordings and an orderly quit

  1. mp4/mov recordings are written fragmented, so a crash or power cut leaves a playable file (mkv was already crash-tolerant; the owned .frec always was). If a session ends uncleanly mid-recording, the next launch offers a one-click repair into a (repaired) copy — the original is never touched.
  2. Quitting while live, recording, or replay-armed shows exactly what will happen, then performs an ordered shutdown: end the stream → finalize the recording → flush the replay buffer → save.
  3. Recording filename templates (Settings → Output): token-based names — {prefix} {date} {time} {scene} {profile} {canvas} {marker-count} {counter} — for recordings, replay saves and stills, with per-output folders for replays and stills.
  4. Export diagnostics (the bug-report dialog): a zip of the config snapshot, encoder/device probe, recent stats and the last crash report — built by a strict allowlist, so stream keys, passwords, file paths and names are never included. You can read the exact content first; nothing is ever sent anywhere.

Sources & devices

The 0.99.0 batch — everything between the glass and the canvas: test signals, sync calibration, data-driven text, timers, the hotkey map, mixer console depth, and real device controls.

Test signals & A/V sync calibration

  1. Add a Test Signal source (Sources → + → Test Signal): SMPTE-style color bars, a calibration grid, a motion sweep, the 1 kHz lineup tone (−20 dBFS), or the combined A/V sync flash+beep pattern — video and audio generated from one clock. Verify a projector, an encoder, or a stream target with no camera plugged in.
  2. Open the A/V sync calibration workbench (palette → "A/V sync calibration…"): pick the camera and microphone to align, point the camera at the flashing program (a fullscreen projector helps — the dialog opens one), and let it measure for ~12 seconds. It detects the flash and beep onsets over several cycles and offers to apply the measured offset to the microphone's sync offset; the manual slider remains the fallback. Honest note: the loop runs through your display and speakers, so their own small latencies are part of what's measured — and failures say why (camera never saw the flash, mic never heard the beep, room events that aren't the pattern).

Timers, clocks, and text from a file

  1. A Timer source shows a wall clock (strftime format, optional fixed UTC offset), a countdown (to a duration or the next wall time — at zero it can flash or switch scene), a stopwatch, or time since live / since recording. Start/pause/reset from the source's Properties, or bind the two global hotkeys that drive all timers at once (Settings → Hotkeys).
  2. A Text source can read from a watched file (Properties → Read from file): the whole .txt, one CSV cell (by header name or index), or one JSON value (an RFC 6901 pointer like /teams/0/score). Changes appear within half a second; atomic-write editors (temp + rename) are tolerated by holding the last good value. Scoreboards and lower thirds with no service.

The hotkey map & the mixer console

  1. The hotkey map (palette → "Hotkey map…") lists every binding — global actions and per-source push-to-talk/push-to-mute — searchable and filterable, with honest conflict signals: the same chord written two ways is flagged as shared, and a key the OS refused to register (owned by another app, or unavailable on this compositor) says so. Export cheat sheet writes a Markdown reference into Downloads.
  2. Every mixer strip now has pan (stereo balance — centered is bit-identical to no pan; double-click resets), PFL solo (the monitor hears only soloed strips; the program mix never changes), and a mono downmix — pan and mono live in the strip's "⋯" popover, solo on the strip face.

Device depth: deinterlacing & camera controls

  1. A Video Capture Device's Properties now offer deinterlacing for interlaced feeds (capture cards fed by older cameras/consoles): discard, bob, linear, blend, or motion-adaptive, with field-order selection. Pure CPU on the capture thread — identical on every OS; changing the mode restarts the device, like a format change.
  2. Camera controls (exposure, white balance, focus, zoom, gain, and more) appear in the same Properties while the device is streaming. Windows and Linux use the native UVC paths; macOS surfaces whatever AVFoundation reports — often nothing, and the panel says so rather than showing fake sliders. Every adjustment saves into a per-device profile that reapplies automatically on hotplug and restart; Reset profile returns the backend defaults.

Automation & control

Make the studio drive itself with rules, macros, and hardware control surfaces — every one of them routed through a single fixed command allowlist so no automation can ever name a file, spawn a process, or reach the internet.

Rules, macros & variables

  1. Open Tools → Automation Rules & Macros… and add a rule: pick one of nine triggers (scene switch, stream state, recording state, source error, audio level crossing, system idle, window focus, time of day, or a watched file changing), add conditions (a variable equals, streaming, recording), and choose a studio action. Every rule ships disabled — enable it to arm it.
  2. Build a macro as a named sequence of steps (run an action, wait, set a variable) with a repeat count and an optional global hotkey; run it from the UI, a hotkey, a rule, or the remote API.
  3. Set a studio variable in a macro and reference it as {{name}} inside any Text source — the text updates live the moment the macro sets the value.

Control surfaces: MIDI, OSC & touch panel

  1. Open Tools → MIDI Control… and MIDI-learn a pad, knob, or fader onto an action, macro, scene switch, mixer fader, or mute — with LED and motor-fader feedback that mirrors the studio's real state. No MIDI port opens until you pick one.
  2. Enable the OSC control surface (Listen for OSC) to let TouchOSC-class controllers and lighting desks send addresses like /scene/switch, /record/start, /macro/run, and /mixer/vol. It is off by default and binds loopback unless LAN is enabled; OSC has no password of its own.
  3. Open Tools → LAN Panel & Tally… to serve a control page (scene buttons with live tally, mixer faders, action keys) to any phone via a QR code — a password is required on every request and it stays loopback unless you enable LAN. The tally page shows full-screen red/green; add ?scene=NAME to watch a single scene.

Hotkey chords & layers

  1. In a hotkey field, bind either a plain key (Ctrl+Shift+M) or a two-stroke chord (Ctrl+K, then 3) — the chord's second key is only claimed while the chord is pending. Assign a Layer so a binding fires only while that layer is active; layers are sticky, so a layer key switches layers and stays there.

Cameras & show rundown

  1. Open Tools → PTZ Cameras…, add a camera by its VISCA-over-IP address, store named pan/tilt/zoom presets, and bind a preset to a scene so going on program auto-recalls that shot. PTZ is LAN-only, and a camera exists only because you entered its address — nothing is discovered.
  2. Open Tools → Show Rundown… and order steps (a scene, how long it holds, plus optional actions) with manual or automatic advance and a live "next up + remaining time". Running a rundown switches scenes the ordinary, undoable way and never edits the scene collection.
Note: Every rule, macro, MIDI/OSC binding, panel button, and rundown step dispatches through one fixed command allowlist — the same one the remote API exposes — so no control surface can reach a command the app's own buttons don't. Every network surface is off by default and binds loopback unless you explicitly enable LAN, and the OSC and LAN-panel settings warn that LAN traffic is unencrypted before the port opens.

Multistream

Stream to several platforms at the same time — direct from your machine, no restream server, completely free.

  1. Add two or more stream targets in Settings → Stream and enable each.
  2. Set per-service encoder settings if a platform needs a different bitrate.
  3. Click Go Live — you broadcast to all enabled targets at once. The stats dock shows each target's health and bitrate.
  4. Optionally record a separate-track local copy at the same time, and save replays with the replay hotkey while you stream.
Free for everyone: every feature — multistream, SRT/WHIP, advanced filters/scripting, replay presets, the stinger packs, and remote-control automation — is free. No paid tier, no payments, no ads.

Streaming reliability & insight

Rehearse, measure, and explain your show: drill against a simulated bad network, run the whole pipeline to a private local sink, review a forensic timeline of every event, benchmark what your machine can sustain, and split source visibility between the stream and the recording. Everything ships off or neutral by default and adds no network call.

Network-condition simulator

  1. In Settings → Stream, open the Network simulator (rehearsals) and choose a Profile — Hotel Wi-Fi, Mobile hotspot, or Custom with your own Bandwidth, Latency, Jitter, and scheduled Outage timing. It shapes only the rehearsal's local sinks so you can drill reconnects, multistream isolation, and bitrate choices; by construction it can never degrade a real Go Live.

Dry-run rehearsal mode

  1. Press the violet Rehearse button to run the whole pipeline — compositor, encoder, mux, and the real reconnect logic — against an owned loopback sink with zero bytes leaving the machine. A REHEARSAL badge and banner mark every live surface, and stream keys are never even read, so a pre-show dry run works before the keys exist. When prompted, you can optionally also record the rehearsal to a video file.
Tip: Pair the dry run with the network simulator to rehearse reconnect behavior against a thin, flapping uplink well before show day.

Dropped-frame forensic timeline

  1. Watch the Session timeline record the whole session — render time, encoder queue depth, per-target bitrate and drops, reconnects, scene switches, markers, and alarms on one zoomable graph. Where the stats dock shows now, this answers "what happened at minute 43?" after the fact, sampled off the render loop so the 60 fps compose path gains no work.

Session report & encoder benchmark

  1. Enable Write a session report (HTML + Markdown) next to the recording in Settings → Stream to get a local write-up on session end: uptime, average and peak bitrate per target, down-time percentage, the reconnect log, markers, alarms, and recording paths. Local only and off by default.
  2. Use Run encoder benchmark… to actually run short measured encode ladders (every detected encoder × preset × resolution) on your machine — about a minute, fully offline — then Apply to recording settings from the measured recommendation. Failures are listed, not hidden, and the benchmark is refused while you're live or recording so it never steals the encoder mid-show.

Per-output source visibility

  1. On any source row, use the hover toggles — Show on stream / Hide on stream and Show in recording / Hide in recording — to keep alerts and chat on the live feed while the VOD recording stays clean, all within a single canvas. The master eye toggle still governs everything, and the operator's preview, projectors, and multiview always show the full program.

Virtual camera

Turn your produced scene into a webcam for any video-call app.

  1. Click Start Virtual Camera in Controls. Freally Capture registers a system camera named "Freally Capture Virtual Camera".
  2. In Zoom, Meet, Discord, or Teams, choose that camera in the video settings — your composed program feed becomes the webcam.
  3. Click Stop Virtual Camera to remove the device.
Linux note: the virtual camera uses v4l2loopback, a kernel module you install once. Freally Capture guides you through it and never installs system components silently. macOS uses a bundled, notarized CoreMediaIO plugin.

Replay buffer

Keep a rolling buffer so a hotkey saves the moment that just happened.

  1. Click Start Replay Buffer in Controls. Freally Capture keeps the last N seconds in a bounded buffer.
  2. Bind a global Save Replay hotkey in Settings.
  3. Press the hotkey to save the last N seconds to a file — without interrupting your stream or main recording. A "Replay saved" toast confirms it.

Teleprompter

A smooth-scrolling script for the talent — on an in-app panel, a mirror-able fullscreen projector, and the LAN touch panel at once. Reworked in 0.910.0 with pause chips, a start countdown, BPM pacing, read-aloud, and autocomplete.

  1. Open the teleprompter from Tools, type or paste your script, and set the speed and font. Pace the scroll in characters per second, or switch to BPM mode (50–250 beats per minute) to read lyrics in time with music — either change re-paces the running scroll live.
  2. Pause chips: type -- for a beat or --N for an N-second hold and it becomes an atomic pill — the caret skips over it, one Delete/Backspace removes the whole marker, and copy/paste carries it intact.
  3. Start countdown: arm an optional 3-2-1 pre-roll; it shows on both the operator preview and the talent projector so the reader gets a beat before the text moves.
  4. Open the projector for the talent (mirror mode flips it for beam-splitter glass). Opening it closes the operator modal, so you can run your livestream on the main window while the talent reads; with a countdown armed the projector auto-rewinds to the top, counts down, and plays. Drive play / pause / stop / rewind / fast-forward / restart from the on-screen transport controls, a hotkey, MIDI, OSC, or the LAN panel — the scroll offset is shared, so every surface stays in step.
  5. Read aloud previews the script through your operating system's own speech synthesis — Windows SAPI/OneCore, macOS AVSpeechSynthesis, Linux spd-say/espeak-ng where installed. No TTS engine is bundled.
  6. Autocomplete offers light-grey word and phrase suggestions as you type; press Tab to accept. Each of the 18 app languages has its own local dictionary, loaded only for the language you're writing in (attributions in THIRD-PARTY.md).

Remote control

Drive the studio from a Stream Deck or Companion-style controller — off by default, password-protected, on your machine.

  1. Open Settings → Remote Control and enable the WebSocket API (off by default). Set a password.
  2. It binds to 127.0.0.1 by default; LAN access is an explicit opt-in.
  3. Point a Companion / Stream Deck client at the shown WebSocket URL. You can switch scenes, commit transitions, toggle mutes, start/stop recording, and save replays from hardware buttons.

Remote guests & show production

Turn a remote-guest session into a produced show: host up to eight guests over an opt-in peer-to-peer connection, draw on the live program, hold guests in a green room, send private cues, monitor each guest's connection, and reflow everyone into an automatic grid. Every new surface is off or unbound by default, and nothing connects until you invite someone.

Multi-guest remote sessions

  1. Invite guests into the opt-in remote-guest session — now host plus up to eight guests, a 1–9 gallery. Each guest is its own tile with its own mute gates, connection quality, cue channel, and one-click seating. Media flows directly peer-to-peer between host and guests: no broker, no server you run, and nothing that auto-connects.
Note: The session is validated to a four-guest performance budget; going beyond that depends on the host's bandwidth and CPU.

Telestrator — draw on the live program

  1. Open the Telestrator and pick a tool — Pen, Highlighter, Arrow, or Ellipse — then draw straight onto the outgoing program. Marks are composited into the program at the moment you draw them, so they land in both the recording and the stream (including the stream-only / recording-only variants), not just your preview.
  2. Let marks Fade after a set time, or switch to Whiteboard mode to Keep them until you clear. Drawing is tablet-pressure aware, and Clear marks is available as a hotkey once you assign it (unbound by default).

Green room

  1. Turn on the Green room so new guests land in a private holding state — you see their camera and a cam/mic tech-check in the Green-room preview, off-program, before they go live. When they're ready, click Seat on air to put them into the show in one step. The guest sees a "you're in the green room" banner on both the desktop app and the web join page.

Cue & talk

  1. Send a connected guest a private Cue — including presets like 30 seconds, Wrap up, You're next, and Speak up — over the existing P2P data channel. The guest sees the cue with a live countdown on their join page or app, never in the program, and it adds no new network surface.

Connection-quality dashboard

  1. Watch the live per-guest QoS roll-up — round-trip time, packet loss, received resolution and framerate, and jitter — with a green/amber/red badge and a history sparkline, so "your connection is degrading" is visible before viewers ever notice.

Auto-grid guest layouts

  1. Enable Auto-grid to keep seated guests arranged in a non-overlapping 1–9 grid that reflows as guests join, leave, or are seated, or press the arrange control to reflow them now. Each seat publishes its name as a {{guestN}} variable, so a title source becomes an auto-updating nameplate.

Studio management & workflow

Make a whole layout portable and shareable, keep versioned checkpoints of your scenes, theme the app, and put your most-used actions one tap away — all strictly local, with no server and no new network calls.

Scene packs & version control

  1. Open the Workspace dialog from Scene Collection → Manage Scene Collections… — the pack, compare, snapshot, backup, and dock-layout tools all live here.
  2. Click Export pack… to bundle the active collection plus its local assets (images, media, LUTs, masks, fonts) into a single portable .fcappack. URL and UNC paths are recorded as external rather than bundled, and fonts are flagged for attribution.
  3. Click Import pack… and pick a .fcappack — it lands as a new collection, relinks its assets on this machine, and switches to it.
  4. Click Compare & merge… to diff the active collection against another collection, a .fcappack, or a snapshot; review added / removed / changed scenes, sources, transforms, filters, and visibility, then cherry-pick exactly which changes to merge in. Picking a scene also brings the sources it uses.
  5. Click Snapshots…, name a checkpoint, and choose Save snapshot. Use Restore later to return to it — your current layout is saved as a snapshot first, so a restore is never one-way. Snapshots are capped at 50 with disk usage shown.
Tip: Snapshots are intentional, named history that sits beside autosave and undo — use them before a risky edit so you always have a labeled point to come back to.

Backup, restore & portable mode

  1. In the Workspace dialog, click Backup & restore…, then Export backup… to save everything — settings, profiles, and scene collections — to one versioned .fcapbackup.
  2. To move to a new machine, click Restore from backup…, tick which parts to bring in (Settings, Scene collections, Profiles), and choose Restore selected. Restart Freally Capture afterward to load restored collections and profiles.
  3. To run from a USB stick, drop a freally-portable.txt file next to the executable — all app data then lives under the exe's folder instead of the host OS profile. Settings → About shows the portable status and the data-folder location.
Note: Stream keys and passwords are never written into a .fcapbackup — re-enter them after restoring. Server-owned fields (EULA acceptance, onboarding, camera profiles) are preserved on restore.

Theming, layout & quick actions

  1. Open Settings → Appearance and pick the Custom theme to reveal the palette editor. Author the full six-colour palette with live WCAG contrast warnings, then use Export .fctheme… / Import .fctheme… to share it as a .fctheme file.
  2. In the Workspace dialog's Dock layouts section, save the current arrangement under a name (a layout remembers stats-dock visibility and mixer orientation) and click any saved layout to switch to it.
  3. In the Settings modal, type in the Search settings… field to jump straight to any setting; a per-category marker flags what has changed since you opened Settings.
  4. Choose Tools → Quick actions… to open the button grid. Click Edit, then Add button, set each button's label and colour, and add Pages. Buttons fire scenes, macros, soundboard pads, and studio commands.
Tip: Quick-action command buttons fire the same allowlisted studio commands as hotkeys, and the grid's config is shared with the LAN touch panel — set it up once and it shows up on your phone too.

Settings

Profiles, scene collections, hotkeys, and language — everything is free, so there is nothing to activate. Since 0.301.0, settings live in a categorised modal (General · Appearance · Streaming · Output · Replay · Hotkeys · Network · Accessibility · About) reached from the menu bar — File · Edit · View · Docks · Profile · Scene Collection · Tools · Help across the top of the window.

  1. Apply means apply. The Settings modal edits a private copy — nothing changes until you click Apply (or OK). Cancel restores exactly what you had, live theme preview included.
  2. Profiles & scene collections: keep separate setups (e.g. "Streaming" vs "Local recording") and switch them without restarting, from the Profile and Scene Collection menus.
  3. Hotkeys: bind every action from a conflict-proof dropdown — pick a shortcut for one action and it disappears from every other action's menu, so two features can never share a key; "None" frees it again.
  4. Accessibility: set the mixer meter colours, including a colour-blind-safe preset.
  5. Language: pick from 18 languages (English first); the UI updates immediately, with full RTL for Arabic.

Privacy

Freally Capture is local-first and account-free. Here is exactly what it does and does not do with your data.

  1. No account, no cloud, no telemetry. The app never requires a login and never sends your scenes, recordings, or any usage data to any server.
  2. Streams go direct. Your broadcast goes from your machine straight to the platforms you configured — there is no restream server we run.
  3. Recordings stay on your device. They are written to the folder you choose. Nothing is uploaded.
  4. Stream keys are local. They are stored on your machine, masked in the UI, and sent only to the service you stream to.
  5. Nothing bundled but the engine. ffmpeg (for the wire codecs the platforms require) and the optional virtual-background model are not bundled — they're fetched on demand and verified, and the app shows you what it will fetch.
  6. For the full policy, see PRIVACY.md on GitHub.
